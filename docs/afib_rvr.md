# Atrial Fibrillation with Rapid Ventricular Response (AFib RVR)

## Overview
Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response is an irregular, often fast heart rhythm
where the ventricular rate exceeds 100 beats per minute, commonly above 120 bpm. It reduces
cardiac output and can cause hypotension, dyspnea, and chest discomfort.

## Rate control
First-line management for a hemodynamically stable patient is rate control. Intravenous
beta-blockers or non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers are used to bring the ventricular
rate below 110 bpm. Check blood pressure before and after administration because these agents can
worsen hypotension.

## Anticoagulation
Atrial fibrillation increases stroke risk from atrial thrombus. Anticoagulation decisions use the
CHA2DS2-VASc score; most patients with a score of 2 or more receive an oral anticoagulant.

## Escalation
If the heart rate remains above 150 bpm despite rate control, or systolic blood pressure falls
below 90 mmHg, escalate to on-call cardiology within 15 minutes and consider synchronized
cardioversion for an unstable patient.

## Monitoring
Continuous ECG monitoring for at least 24 hours. Recheck heart rate every 15 minutes for the
first hour, then hourly once controlled.
